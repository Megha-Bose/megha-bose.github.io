The Tape, the Pen, and the Camera

Jun 2026
4 min read

There is an image I return to when I think about time. A person sitting at a desk in a dark room. On the left, a screen plays back footage - slightly grainy, the kind that flickers at the edges. A camera stands on a tripod, pointing towards them. And the person at the desk is writing.

The screen is the past.

What is on it cannot be changed. The footage ran when it ran, was captured when it was captured. You can rewind it, pause it, watch certain moments twice. You can study the decisions you made and what happened. But you cannot reach into the screen and change what it shows. The tape is already recorded. What happened, happened - and it exists now as something you observe rather than something you are still inside.

This is a more useful framing than most of what I have been told about the past. Don’t dwell on it, people say. Learn from it and move on. But that’s not quite precise enough. The thing is you are the audience of the past. Not the director, not the editor, not one of the actors who can call for another take. You are in the seat, watching. And from that seat, you can take notes.

The actions, the writing - the notes - that is the present.

What you are writing right now is shaped by what you are watching. The past informs it. But the writing is happening in its own time, under your hand, word by word. It is not the screen. It is not necessarily dependent on the screen. The footage shows what it shows and the page receives what it receives, and these are related but not the same thing.

The present is the only tense in which anything actually happens. You are not watching the tape while also waiting for it to change, nor are you writing the final version of something that will outlast everything. You are just writing. That is enough. The present does not need to be larger than it is.

The camera captures the present which you will watch later.

But, you are not the cinematographer. You are not in control of what the camera captures, who else walks into the frame, what light falls, what the reel eventually shows. You can point it in a direction. You can decide what to say, what to build, what to send out into the room. But you are a co-author of what the footage becomes.

This is not nihilism. Co-authorship is real authorship - you are genuinely part of what gets made. The things you write now, the choices you put into the world, influence the footage even if they do not dictate it.

But you are not the only input. And the output is not yours to know in advance.

What this does, for me, is demystify a lot of what used to feel enormous. Regret assumes you were the director of a scene you could have reshot. Anxiety about the future assumes you are the sole author of something that is actually being written by many hands, including forces that have nothing to do with you. Disappointment assumes the outcome was yours to control in the first place - but you were only ever a co-author. The rest was always someone else’s chapter to write. None of these assumptions hold up.

The past is not a verdict. It is a recording. The future is not a destination you reach alone. It is something being written collaboratively, with you as one contributor among many. And the present is the desk between the two - the only place where anything can actually be done.

You are the observer of the past. You are the present. You are the co-author of the future.

The small things that felt so heavy tend to get lighter when you are clear about which role you are in.
